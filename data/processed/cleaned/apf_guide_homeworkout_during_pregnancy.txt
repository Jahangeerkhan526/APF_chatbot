Choose your workouts
carefully to make sure
they are appropriate for
you.
  You don't need any
special kit but consider
wearing a supportive bra,
sensible shoes and bump
supporting leggings.
Take time to warm up and
cool down.
Take a break as and when
you need to.
If you are new to
resistance training, start
gently with bodyweight,
bands and light weights
and keep within your
limits.
Your workout space
should be free from
obstructions, not
slippery, well ventilated,
and heated at the right
temperature.
Reduce high impact
activities (such as
jumping) which put extra
strain on the pelvic floor,
as pregnancy progresses.
Breathe out through
the hardest part of
movement.
Eat well and drink
regularly before and
throughout the session to
prevent dehydration and
overheating.
Wear comfortable loose
clothing - consider
layering to accommodate
for changing room and
body temperature.
Avoid exercises lying flat on your
back after the first trimester.
Don't continue with certain
exercises or workouts if they
make you feel uncomfortable.
Don't hold your breath whilst
lifting weights.
Don't push yourself to your limit,
focus on moving well and feeling
good.
Don't continue to train through
pain, lightheadedness, breathing
difficulties, contractions, vaginal
bleeding, amniotic fluid leakage,
or any other unusual symptoms.
Stop and seek advice.
Avoid or modify movements where
you are unable to find balance or
overstretches you.

TOP TIPS
Do your homework - when choosing
an online workout be sure to
check that the instructor holds
the necessary pre- and postnatal
qualification and can therefore adapt
activities as required.
Now more than ever you will
need to listen to your body,
especially if the online activity is
recorded, or when you are unable
to talk directly to the instructor
to ask for guidance.
It's never too early or too late to
start pelvic floor exercises. Try and
incorporate these as part of your daily
routine.
You're in charge, so adjust
rest periods, frequency and
repetitions as as you feel
necessary.
Being active with your partner and/
or kids can be a great way of spending
time together, but also sets a good
example and helps to normalise an
active lifestyle, which is beneficial to
everyone!
As a golden rule, if it feels
comfortable keep going, if it's not
adapt, or stop and seek advice.

AFTER CHILDBIRTH
Give your body time to heal, prioritise
rest and recovery in the early weeks.
If you've had a straightforward birth,
you can start gentle exercises as soon
as you feel up to it. This could include
walking, gentle stretches, pelvic floor
exercises and exercises that target the
deepest layer of the abdominal muscles.
If you've had a caesarean or any surgical
intervention and/or complications, your
recovery time will be longer.

Breastfeeding when being active at a
moderate intensity or when taking part
in strength training is safe and does not
impact on breast milk quality or infant
growth. Expressing or feeding your baby
before working out is recommended.
Also consider investing in a suitable bra
that will give you support whilst training
and remember to keep breast pads with
you to manage any leaking that can
occur.
The 6-8 week postnatal check is a
good time to discuss physical activity
with your healthcare professional.
