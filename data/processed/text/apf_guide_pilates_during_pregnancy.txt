DO’s
Wear comfortable loose 
clothing – consider 
layering to accommodate 
for changing room and 
body temperature.
Listen to your body 
and ask the instructor if 
something doesn’t feel 
right.
Find a qualified pre- 
and postnatal Pilates 
instructor who can 
support and guide you 
with modifications  
throughout pregnancy 
and beyond.
Eat well and drink 
regularly before and 
throughout the session to 
prevent dehydration and 
overheating.
Wear a supportive bra 
and consider bump 
supporting leggings or 
belt.
Make movements smaller 
and/or modify exercises 
as needed – ask your 
instructor for guidance.
Take a break as and when 
you need to.
DON’Ts
Don’t force movements. Be kind 
to yourself as some exercises will 
feel different/more challenging 
during pregnancy.
Avoid movements which involve 
lying flat on your back after the 
first trimester.
Don’t hold your breath whilst 
completing the exercises.
Avoid changing positions too 
quickly - don’t sit down or stand 
up too quickly.
Avoid ‘overstretching’ and 
‘pushing through’ a class as you 
may have done before pregnancy.
Don’t continue the class through 
pain, lightheadedness, breathing 
difficulties, contractions, vaginal 
bleeding, amniotic fluid leakage or 
any other unusual symptoms. Stop 
and seek advice.
Avoid overheating and extreme 
heat conditions, e.g. Hot Pilates.

TOP TIPS
Find a a qualified pre- and postnatal 
instructor who is the right fit for 
you, so you can look forward to 
class.
Rolling onto your side and walking 
up through the hands when coming 
up to standing from the floor can 
help to avoid unnecessary intra-
abdominal pressure.
Take time to master the technique 
of diaphragmatic breathing i.e. 
when the diaphragm, deep core and 
pelvic floor work together, as this 
will help to maintain core strength.
Pilates can be great if suffering 
from pelvic girdle pain, but  
remember to inform your 
instructor so they can modify 
exercises where necessary. Seeking 
help and advice from a specialist 
pelvic health physiotherapist is also 
recommended.
Pay attention to what your tummy 
looks like when you’re doing the 
exercises - doming or coning may 
indicate that the movement needs 
to be modified.

AFTER CHILDBIRTH
Give your body time to heal, prioritise 
rest and recovery in the early weeks. 
If you’ve had a straightforward birth, 
you can start gentle exercises as soon 
as you feel up to it. This could include 
walking, gentle stretches, pelvic floor 
exercises and exercises that target the 
deepest layer of the abdominal muscles. 
If you’ve had a caesarean or any surgical 
intervention and/or complications, your 
recovery time will be longer.
Breastfeeding, when being active at 
a moderate intensity or when taking 
part in strength training, is safe and 
does not impact on breast milk quality 
or infant growth. It is recommended 
that you express or feed your baby 
before taking part in a Pilates class. Also 
consider investing in a suitable bra that 
will give you support whilst training and 
remember to keep breast pads with you 
to manage any leaking that can occur.
The 6-8 week postnatal check is a 
good time to discuss physical activity 
with your healthcare professional.  
Reintroduce activities gradually and 
focus on technique, breathing, pelvic 
floor and abdominal function. If you need 
any guidance on these activities, you 
may wish to arrange a 1:1 session with a 
qualified instructor.
If you are breastfeeding, it may be 
uncomfortable to perform exercises in a 
prone position. You may need to modify 
exercises or ask your instructor if you 
need guidance on how to do this.
You should only return to higher intensity 
activities once you have recovered from 
childbirth and in the absence of any 
signs or symptoms of pelvic floor 
and/or abdominal wall dysfunction. 
Consider seeking the support of a 
specialist pelvic health physiotherapist 
during this time.
